Place
Nalanda Mahavihara
Bihar
Nalanda Mahavihara, located in Bihar, India, is an ancient Buddhist monastic university and a UNESCO World Heritage site. Established in the 5th century CE, it thrived as a major center of learning for over 700 years, attracting scholars from across Asia. The site includes stupas, shrines, viharas, and extensive manuscripts, reflecting a comprehensive curriculum that covered theology, philosophy, medicine, and more. Nalanda Mahavihara is a symbol of India's rich educational heritage and its historical significance in Buddhist studies.
Place
Mahabodhi Temple
Bihar
The Mahabodhi Temple in Bodh Gaya, Bihar, India, is a UNESCO World Heritage site and a revered Buddhist pilgrimage destination. It marks the spot where Siddhartha Gautama, the Buddha, attained enlightenment around 531 BCE. Originally built by Emperor Ashoka in the 3rd century BCE and later reconstructed, the temple features a towering pyramidal spire and houses a descendant of the original Bodhi Tree. The Mahabodhi Temple is an exemplary piece of ancient Indian architecture and holds immense spiritual and cultural significance.
Place
Vaishali
Bihar
Vaishali, located in the state of Bihar, India, is an ancient city of great historical and cultural significance. It is considered one of the earliest republics in the world, with a democratic governance system dating back to around 600 BCE. Vaishali holds a prominent place in Buddhist and Jain traditions; it was here that Lord Buddha delivered his last sermon and announced his impending Nirvana, and it is also associated with Lord Mahavira, the 24th Tirthankara of Jainism. The city is marked by several archaeological sites, including the Ashokan Pillar, which commemorates Emperor Ashoka's conversion to Buddhism. Vaishali's rich heritage reflects its importance as a center of ancient Indian civilization, politics, and religion.
Place
Cyclopean Wall of Rajgir
Bihar
The Cyclopean Wall of Rajgir is an ancient stone wall surrounding the city of Rajgir in Bihar, India. Built during the Mauryan period, around the 3rd century BCE, it stretches over 40 kilometers and is constructed using large, unworked stones without the use of mortar, a technique reminiscent of the Cyclopean walls found in ancient Greece. The wall served as a fortification for the ancient city, which was the capital of the Magadha Empire and an important center for both Buddhism and Jainism. The Cyclopean Wall is a testament to the engineering skills of ancient Indian builders and offers valuable insights into the military architecture and urban planning of the time. It stands as a significant archaeological and cultural heritage site in India.
