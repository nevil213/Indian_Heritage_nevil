Place
Red Fort
Delhi
The Red Fort, located in Delhi, India, is a UNESCO World Heritage site and a symbol of India's rich Mughal heritage. Constructed by Emperor Shah Jahan in 1648, this grand fort served as the main residence of the Mughal emperors for nearly 200 years. The fort's massive red sandstone walls enclose a complex of palaces, pavilions, and gardens, showcasing a blend of Persian, Timurid, and Indian architectural styles. Notable structures include the Diwan-i-Aam (Hall of Public Audience) and the Diwan-i-Khas (Hall of Private Audience). The Red Fort is a historic landmark and a venue for India's Independence Day celebrations.
Place
Qutub Minar
Delhi
The Qutub Minar, a soaring 13th-century tower in Delhi, is a UNESCO treasure. This victory tower, commemorating Islamic rule, is a blend of Indian and Islamic architecture, built with red sandstone and intricate carvings. Part of the Qutb complex, it stands beside the Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque and an enigmatic iron pillar. A testament to craftsmanship and cultural exchange, the Qutub Minar endures as a majestic heritage site.
Place
Humayun's Tomb
Delhi
Humayun's Tomb in Delhi, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is a precursor to the Taj Mahal. Built in the 16th century for Mughal Emperor Humayun, it's an exquisite example of Mughal architecture. The red sandstone tomb with its white marble dome and intricate details blends Persian and Indian styles. The surrounding gardens, following the charbagh (four-quartered) layout, add to the serene beauty. Humayun's Tomb is a testament to the evolution of Mughal architectural design and its lasting influence.
